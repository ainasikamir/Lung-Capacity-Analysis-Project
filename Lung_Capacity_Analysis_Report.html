Models to explore the nature of the relationship between variables

The null hypothesis in the context of ANOVA tests for model comparison typically states that the reduced model (the simpler model being compared) is sufficient to explain the variation in the response variable, and there is no significant improvement in model fit by adding additional terms.

Model 1 vs Model 2:

Null hypothesis: The simpler model (Model 1) with only the linear term for height is sufficient to explain the variation in lung capacity. Adding the quadratic term (Model 2) does not significantly improve the model fit.

Model 2 vs Model 3:

Null hypothesis: The model with the linear and quadratic terms (Model 2) is sufficient to explain the variation in lung capacity. Adding the cubic term (Model 3) does not significantly improve the model fit beyond what is captured by the linear and quadratic terms.
In both cases, the alternative hypothesis is that adding the additional terms significantly improves the model fit. The p-value associated with the F-statistic in the ANOVA output indicates the probability of observing such a significant improvement in model fit under the null hypothesis. If the p-value is below a chosen significance level (e.g., 0.05), we reject the null hypothesis in favor of the alternative hypothesis, concluding that the more complex model provides a significant improvement in explaining the variation in the response variable.

Model 1 vs Model 2:

The F-statistic is significant (F = 58.91, p < 0.05), indicating that Model 2, with the quadratic term, significantly improves the model fit compared to Model 1.

Therefore, the quadratic term adds explanatory power to the model, leading to a better fit.

Therefore, we reject the null hypothesis and conclude that Model 2 provides a significant improvement in explaining the variation in lung capacity compared to Model 1.

Model 2 vs Model 3:

The F-statistic is not significant (F = 0.196, p > 0.05), suggesting that Model 3, with the cubic term, does not provide a significant improvement in fit compared to Model 2.

Therefore, including the cubic term in Model 3 does not significantly enhance the model beyond the quadratic term in Model 2.

Therefore, we fail to reject the null hypothesis, suggesting that Model 3 does not provide a significant improvement in explaining the variation in lung capacity beyond what is captured by Model 2.

Conclusion

Model 2 and Model 3 both show improvements in R-squared compared to Model 1, suggesting better fit.

However, Model 3 does not seem to provide a significant improvement over Model 2, as indicated by the similarity in their R-squared values and the non-significant coefficients for the cubic term in Model 3.

In summary, both Model 2 and Model 3 provide better fits than Model 1, but there's no significant improvement from Model 2 to Model 3. Therefore, Model 2 may be preferred due to its simplicity unless there's a theoretical reason to include the cubic term.
